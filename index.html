What is an IPO?

An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is the very first time a private company offers its shares to the general public, transitioning to a publicly traded company.

This process is often called "going public." It's a major milestone, enabling a company to raise significant capital for growth, expansion, or other strategic goals.

This is where the IPO happens. Investors buy shares **directly from the company** (via its underwriters) at a fixed offering price set before trading begins. Think of it as the "new issue" market, where securities are created and sold for the first time.

This is the regular stock market (like the NYSE or Nasdaq) where shares are traded **between investors** after the IPO. The company is no longer directly involved in these transactions. Prices here fluctuate constantly based on supply and demand.

An Investor's Look at IPOs

For individual investors, IPOs can be an exciting opportunity but come with unique risks and challenges, especially regarding access. Understanding this landscape is key to making informed decisions.

The Challenge of Access

Getting shares at the initial IPO price is difficult for most individuals. Underwriters typically allocate large blocks of shares to institutional investors (like hedge funds and mutual funds) and their high-net-worth clients. While some brokerages offer limited access to retail investors, it's often competitive and not guaranteed.

The Cost of an IPO

Going public is expensive. Costs are significant and are typically deducted from the IPO proceeds. The largest portion is underwriting fees, but legal, accounting, and regulatory fees also make up a substantial part of the total expense. Hover over the chart to see the typical cost breakdown.

Beyond these initial costs, companies face recurring expenses for ongoing compliance, financial reporting (like Sarbanes-Oxley), and investor relations, which can be a significant long-term financial commitment.
